1) Thermal instability in an expanding box with cooling and heating effects

(Student: Manish Kumar Tamta, Research Supervisor: Dr. Prateek Sharma, 2022)


The gas surrounding galaxies outside their disks and inside virial radii is what we call a Circumgalactic medium (CGM). CGM is a multi-phase medium and a source of star-forming fuel. The physics of Gas inflow and outflow in the CGM can tell us how the galaxy will evolve.

To understand the galaxy evolution, it is very crucial to understand the multiphase nature of the CGM. That's where Thermal instability (TI) comes in, which is responsible for the presence of multiphase gas in the CGM. TI arises due to radiative cooling and it has a larger growth rate than that of gravitational instability.

Due to inconsistency between predications from cooling flow models and observed star formation rate, it becomes clear that there should be some form of heating. AGN feedback is one energetically plausible source for such heating.

Paper: Star formation near the sun is driven by the expansion of the local bubble (Zucker el. al.)

(Supernova-driven star formation)

Link for the paper: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-021-04286-5


What is a local bubble?

Def: It is a cavity of low density, high-temperature plasma which is surrounded by a shell of cold neutral gas and dust.

However, Zucker et. al. are uncertain about the shape and time scale for its formation due to the low-resolution model of ISM. They did an analysis of 3d positions and motion of dense gas within 200 pc from the sun. The surface of the local bubble is shown as a closed surface in the following figure but it could be a galactic chimney.

Figure-1

The local bubble surface shows sheet-like or filamentary morphology which is uniformly elongated along the bubble’s surface. The point to be noted here is that bubble-bubble collision causes this kind of morphology. The local bubble lies at the closest distance between the Radcliffe wave (shown as red in Figure-1) and the split (blue). The green sphere is Per-Tau superbubble. (Just info)

The group has used the measurement of 3d positions and motions of stellar clusters in order to reconstruct the star formation history. As previous results show that the 3d motion of the youngest clusters (<~3 Myr) can probe the motion of the gas clouds which are essential for star formation. From the new Gaia EDR 3 estimates of cluster's 3d velocities and more accurate peculiar motion of the sun, they find out that UCL and LCC clusters coincide with the center of the bubble at its birth. Also, the sun entered the local bubble ~5 Myr ago. On the basis of the amount of momentum injection required by SN to sweep up the total mass of the shell ~1.4×10^6 M_sun, they estimated that ~15 (+11/-7) SNs are required to form the local bubble. Zucker et. al. assumed that each star-forming molecular cloud formed because of the shell's expansion and fit the temporal and radial evolution of the local bubble using idealized spherical shell expansion models.

Findings: All of the star-forming material lie on the surface of the local bubble and young stars show outward expansion normal to the surface of the bubble.

  • The local bubble formed due to SN at the center about ~14Myr ago.

  • Age of the local bubble ~14.4 Myr

  • The duration between supernova explosions, powering its expansion is ~ 1.1 Myr

  • The ambient density of ISM before the first SN explosion was ~ 2.7 cm^-3

  • Current radius ~165 +/- 6 pc
