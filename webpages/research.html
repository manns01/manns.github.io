Thermal Instability in a nutshell

  • Given small density perturbations, cooler/denser regions cool further and hotter/low-density regions become further hot in the presence of radiative cooling and heating. This instability is known as thermal instability (TI).
  • George B. Field in his 1965 paper introduced TI as “condensations, not due to gravitation”.


How do we know that such instability should exist in the first place?

  • The existence of a class of astronomical objects i.e. solar prominences, interstellar clouds, and condensations in planetary nebula cannot be explained by self-gravity!


Motivation

  • We find multiphase gas in the galactic environments and clumpiness in the galactic outflows which may be a consequence of thermal instability!

Why should one care?

  • The study of thermal instability in an expanding box tries to address whether the multiphase gas condenses out in a dynamic environment.
  • The bigger question is the source of multiphase gas observed in galactic outflows such as M 82.

Relevant time scales ---> Cooling time, sound crossing time, expansion time


Our Setup: Comoving box model

  • We use idealized numerical simulations to study local thermal instability in a galactic wind.
  • A local cartesian box moving with the galactic wind at a constant velocity only experiences expansion in the directions, orthogonal to the wind! Hence we need an anisotropically expanding box along two directions that mimics the galactic outflow.
  • We use the PLUTO code (Mignone et. al. 2007) to solve the fluid equations.
